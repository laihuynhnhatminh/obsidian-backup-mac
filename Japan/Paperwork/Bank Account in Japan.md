## When to Do It

As soon as possible after arriving — ideally within the **first 1–2 weeks**. You'll need a Japanese bank account for:

- Receiving your salary
- Paying rent (usually via bank transfer)
- Setting up direct debits for utilities, insurance, phone
- Remitting money home to Vietnam

---

## What You Need (Most Banks)

- [ ] **Passport**
- [ ] **Residence Card (在留カード)**
- [ ] **Proof of address (住民票)** — usually required; get this from the ward office or convenience store kiosk
- [ ] A Japanese phone number (needed for online banking and SMS verification)

> ⚠️ **Some banks require you to have lived in Japan for 6 months** before opening an account. Japan Post Bank is the most foreigner-friendly for new arrivals.

---

## Recommended Banks for New Foreigners

### 🟡 Japan Post Bank (ゆうちょ銀行) — Best First Option

- Most foreigner-friendly — will usually open accounts for new arrivals
- Branches in every post office — very accessible in Sapporo
- ATM network at post offices and convenience stores (7-Eleven)
- Lower tech features but rock-solid reliability

### 🔵 Seven Bank (セブン銀行)

- Opens accounts online — quite foreigner-friendly
- Integrated with 7-Eleven ATMs everywhere
- Good for basic use and international transactions

### 🟢 Rakuten Bank / Sony Bank / PayPay Bank (Online Banks)

- More competitive exchange rates for international remittances
- Often require 6 months of residence before opening
- Better for once you're settled in

### 🔴 Hokkaido Bank / North Pacific Bank (道銀 / 北洋銀行)

- Local Hokkaido banks — good for local utility payments, some employers prefer these for salary transfers
- Check if your employer specifies a bank

## For Sending Money Home: Wise

Set up **Wise (formerly TransferWise)** for remittances to Vietnam. Significantly cheaper than bank wire transfers.

- Uses real mid-market exchange rates
- Fees typically 0.4–1.5% vs 2–5% at banks
- Link your Japanese bank account once it's open
- Vietnamese dong (VND) is supported
- Website: https://wise.com

---

## Things to Know About Japanese Banking

- **Hanko (印鑑)**: A personal stamp/seal traditionally used instead of a signature. Some banks still require one; others accept signatures. Japan Post Bank accepts signatures.
- **ATM hours**: Many bank ATMs have **operating hours** and charge fees on nights/weekends. 7-Bank (in 7-Eleven) is 24/7 and free for Japan Post Bank withdrawals.
- **Online banking**: Set up immediately — you'll need it to check transfers, pay bills, and verify payslips
- **Cash culture**: Japan is still heavily cash-based. Always keep ¥10,000–30,000 on hand.
